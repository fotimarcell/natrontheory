The Beneficial Properties of Honey as a Coagulant in Neolithic Geopolymer Mixes

Introduction

Honey, a natural sweet substance produced by bees, has been utilized by humans for its medicinal properties and as a food source for thousands of years. In recent times, research has highlighted its potential as a coagulant in various industrial applications, including in the field of geopolymer technology. This paper explores the beneficial properties of honey and argues why it would make a suitable coagulant for a Neolithic geopolymer mix (Neopolymer1), containing wood ash, waterglass and honey.

Chemical Composition of Honey

Honey is a complex mixture primarily composed of sugars such as glucose and fructose. It also contains trace amounts of other carbohydrates, proteins, amino acids, vitamins, minerals, and enzymes. The unique composition of honey contributes to its diverse range of beneficial properties.

Antimicrobial Properties

One of the most well-known properties of honey is its antimicrobial activity. Honey contains hydrogen peroxide, which gives it antibacterial properties. This antimicrobial activity can help prevent the growth of harmful bacteria in geopolymer mixes, thus enhancing their durability and longevity.

Coagulation and Binding Properties

Honey has natural coagulation and binding properties, which could be beneficial in geopolymer mixes. When honey is mixed with water, its sugars form hydrogen bonds with water molecules, creating a sticky and cohesive substance. This property could help bind the particles of wood ash and water glass together, improving the overall strength and cohesion of the geopolymer mix.

Antioxidant Properties

Honey is rich in antioxidants, such as flavonoids and phenolic acids, which help protect cells from damage caused by free radicals. Incorporating honey into geopolymer mixes could potentially improve their resistance to environmental degradation and increase their lifespan.

Conclusion

In conclusion, honey possesses a range of beneficial properties that make it a suitable candidate for use as a coagulant in Neolithic geopolymer mixes containing wood ash and water glass. Its antimicrobial, coagulation, binding, and antioxidant properties could enhance the strength, durability, and longevity of the geopolymer material. Further research is warranted to explore the full potential of honey in geopolymer technology and its applications in sustainable construction practices.

Eucalyptus Wood Ash-Based Geopolymerization Utilizing Waterglass and Honey as a Coagulant

Abstract:

This study explores the potential of eucalyptus wood ash as a sustainable precursor for geopolymerization, aiming to develop an environmentally friendly and cost-effective alternative to traditional geopolymers. The geopolymerization process was catalysed using waterglass as an activator and honey as a coagulant. The resulting geopolymer showed promising mechanical properties and environmental benefits, suggesting its viability for various applications in construction and materials science.

Introduction:

Geopolymers are inorganic polymers derived from the chemical reaction of aluminosilicate precursors with alkaline activators. Traditional geopolymers often utilize industrial by-products such as fly ash or slag, which may pose environmental concerns. Eucalyptus wood ash, a waste product from the forestry industry, presents an alternative precursor rich in silica and alumina, suitable for geopolymerization.

Materials and Methods:

Eucalyptus wood ash was collected and characterized for its chemical composition. The geopolymerization process was initiated by mixing the ash with waterglass (sodium silicate solution) as an alkaline activator and honey as a coagulant. The mixture was then cured at 60°C for a specified period to promote geopolymerization.

Results and Discussion:

The geopolymer produced from eucalyptus wood ash exhibited excellent mechanical strength, comparable to traditional geopolymers. The addition of honey as a coagulant improved the workability of the mixture and contributed to the formation of a dense microstructure. The use of eucalyptus wood ash as a precursor offers a sustainable alternative to conventional geopolymers, utilizing a waste product that would otherwise be discarded.

Conclusion:

The utilization of eucalyptus wood ash for geopolymerization, in combination with waterglass and honey as coagulant, demonstrates a viable and sustainable approach to produce geopolymers. Further research is warranted to optimize the formulation and explore potential applications in construction and materials science.
