But wood ash is gray, right?

True, wood ash is almost always gray, and it turns the stone gray (or even black) as well. But ancient Egyptian stone objects come in every possible color. You can clearly see the grains of the rock. So we’ll need some kind of transparent additive, not wood ash.

And the ingredient can’t be white either, because that would tint the stone white. The grains have to remain visible.

What could it be?

Acids?

Waterglass is alkaline. If you add any kind of acid to waterglass, it precipitates silica gel — a completely transparent glue that hardens into stone when exposed to air. It could be an ideal binder for creating artificial stone.

At least in theory. But no matter what acid I tried—hydrochloric acid, orange juice, vinegar, etc.—the resulting stone didn’t resemble any natural stone. Do you see this example? It’s like it’s made of jelly.

It is beautiful though, and the Nespresso capsule pressed into it gives it an extra charm. But still...

Alcohol?

Alcohols also immediately precipitate the silica gel from waterglass, and for a long time I thought alcohol would be the ultimate solution—after all, it’s good for hangovers, so why wouldn’t it work here?

In the video, we can see how the silica gel instantly separates from the waterglass when an alcoholic drink is added. However, even the alcoholic gel didn’t produce a stone that resembled a natural one.

Okay, what else?

A base?

But we’ve already crossed bases off the list, since bases don’t destabilize waterglass. I won’t beat around the bush—even though it took me two years—the secret additive is a base.

Specifically, slaked lime, but in a very small, 2% dosage, added as a catalyst only. See, it’s alkaline, so in theory it shouldn’t work at all, and it’s white, so it’s not transparent—but by the time the material sets, it becomes clear.

Artificial fake granite

And this is what the final result looks like. This beautiful cast stone isn’t even made from granite rubble, but from plain quartz sand. The ultimate appearance, of course, depends on the type of ground stone—if it’s lemon yellow or mottled, the stone will look the same.

The binder is transparent. Oh, and it’s hard—Mohs 6–6.5. Oh, and it has the same chemical composition as quartz: SiO₂.

But where did the crushed granite come from?

The usual naysayer complaint is always, ‘How did the Egyptians produce all that crushed granite?’ I’ll tell you. They didn’t. Around Aswan, on the east bank of the Nile, there’s a stone desert.

More precisely: a desert of granite rubble. Yeah. Where even the sand is not sand but crushed granite. Yeah. The pink area is. All you need is a shovel and a broom...
