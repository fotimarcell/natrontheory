Potassium Waterglass

☠️Important safety warning☠️

This process involves potassium hydroxide (KOH), a highly corrosive strong base that can cause severe chemical burns, blindness, or respiratory damage if mishandled. Dissolving it in water releases intense heat (exothermic reaction), which can cause boiling/splattering. Boiling the mixture produces hazardous fumes and increases risks. This is extremely dangerous without proper protective equipment (goggles, gloves, respirator), ventilation, and experience. It is not recommended for home experimentation—serious injury or property damage can occur. Do not attempt this if you're not trained in handling caustic chemicals.

Ingredients

  • 200 grams of Potassium Hydroxide flakes (from a DIY shop where they sell soap making kits)
  • 300 grams of Silica Cat Litter, must be pure silica, SiO2!
  • 900 ml water

Let's cook!



Pour the 900 ml of water into a pot, then add the 200 grams of potassium hydroxide, and finally the 300 grams of cat litter.

It will seem like it will never dissolve, but yes, after about a quarter of an hour of boiling...
...there's hardly any cat litter left. Then continue boiling it until the surface starts to form a skin. If it doesn't want to boil, put a lid on it. The thicker your water glass is, the denser, heavier, and more durable your artificial stone will be.

The beautiful purple color appeared because of the dye from the blue-painted indicator granules. I don't usually pick out the blue granules; the purple waterglass doesn't particularly bother me. In fact, I think it's beautiful!
I sometimes boil it until the silica starts precipitating again at the bottom of the pot (it’s not the skin). That's the maximum density you can achieve under such primitive conditions—about 40% waterglass.

Before pouring it into a jar, let it cool down—not just to avoid sloshing hot water glass around, but also because some of the precipitated waterglass (the skin) will dissolve back into the liquid.
