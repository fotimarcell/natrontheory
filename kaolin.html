Fourth domino
How to transport giant granite statues?

Abu Simbel

We have a great example showing how modern man is capable of transporting ancient, multi-ton granite colossi carved from a single stone from point A to B. This occurred during the construction of the Aswan Dam, where several ancient Egyptian temples had to be saved from flooding. In 1963, professionals transported two stone temples at Abu Simbel.

They also had to move these four massive granite statues located at the entrance of the temple.

The technology behind

The technology used was as follows: they lifted the temple and the statues with heavy machinery and helicopters... Oh wait, no.

They actually cut them into 807 pieces. Modern man doesn't have any other technology to move these hundred-ton statues other than by cutting them up and moving them piece by piece. This is exactly what happened at Abu Simbel.

How ancient Egyptians did it?

Well, there are countless huge, single-piece granite statues and blocks, some weighing several hundred tons, scattered throughout Egypt, which have been shown to come from granite sourced 800 km away. How is that possible?

Well, there is a wall painting where we see the Egyptians dragging a huge statue on a wooden sledge across the sand. One person is pouring water in front of the sledge onto the sand to facilitate sliding.

Case is closed ?

It's certain that the method above works for small distances. You could drag it all the way to a ship.

However, it is problematic that the largest known ancient Egyptian cargo ship, which was recontructed by British Museum's archaeologists, has only 17 tons of capacity. With this, they couldn't even transport 20-ton stones.

Could they have had larger ships? Yes, of course. Let's say we haven't found them. However, this theory does not hinge on that.

Any proof of ancient waterglass use?

Returning to the original mural, we can see a white pile of something accumulating in the background. That something is contaminated with black specks.

Of course, it's contaminated because it's mixed with the remaining charcoal. Then this white substance is carried around in bags, poured out, and someone molds a wall out of it.

All this is depicted in a singe mural (of smelting metal)!
Every ancient civilization with access to natron knew how to make waterglass. We see evidence of this everywhere (scoopmarks). And undoubtedly, they had access to wood ash. The question is whether they tried to mix the two. If they did, they could have created artificial stone. Let's recap what we know so far:
  • Molten natron etches quartz, consequently, granite
  • Etching granite produces waterglass
  • Waterglass is the main component of geopolymers
