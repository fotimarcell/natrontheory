Fourth domino
How to transport giant stone blocks?

Abu Simbel

We have a great example showing how modern man is capable of transporting ancient, multi-ton sandstone colossi carved from a single stone from point A to B. This occurred during the construction of the Aswan Dam, where several ancient Egyptian temples had to be saved from flooding. In 1963, professionals transported two stone temples at Abu Simbel.

They also had to move these four massive statues carved from a single rock located at the entrance of the temple.

The technology behind

The technology used was as follows: they lifted the temple and the statues with heavy machinery and helicopters... Oh wait, no.

They actually cut them into 807 pieces. Eight hundred and seven.

Modern man doesn't have any other technology to move these hundred-ton statues other than by cutting them up and moving them piece by piece. This is exactly what happened at Abu Simbel.

How ancient Egyptians did it?

Well, there are quite a few huge, single-piece granite statues and blocks, some weighing several hundred tons, scattered throughout Egypt, which have been shown to come from granite sourced 800 km away. How is that possible?

Well, there is a wall painting where we see the Egyptians dragging a huge statue on a wooden sledge across the sand. One person is pouring water in front of the sledge onto the sand to facilitate sliding.

Put the stone on a ship!

It's certain that the method above works for small distances. You could drag it all the way to a ship, and you can take the stone as far as you want by boat.

However, the largest known ancient Egyptian cargo ship, which was recontructed by British Museum's archaeologists, has only 17 tons of capacity. With this, they couldn't even transport 20-ton stones.

Could they have had 5-10 times larger ships? Yes, of course. Just because we didn't find them doesn't prove they couldn't have existed. So far so good.

The Serapeum

In Saqqara, there's a place called the Serapeum, where underground caverns house giant stone sarcophagi, each weighing 40 tons, made from noble stones like granite, basalt, and diorite.

This is fascinating because we just saw how many people and how much space is needed to move a 20-ton statue. Well, that was not possible here. These sarcophagi were not brought in this way.

If we discard both the UFO and the Harry Potter theories, we don't have many choices left to explain this...

Did someone say buckets?

However, there's an additional possibility for how all this could have been done. If the Egyptians knew about artificial stones, they could have brought the raw materials easily into the tunnel in buckets and cast the sarcophagi on site.

So, either they didn't move these 40-ton boxes around at all, or they did it with buckets, depending on your perspective.

That is, if they even knew the artificial stone technology.

The modern geopolymer

To evaluate this, let's review what modern geopolymers, or artificial stones, are made of.
  1. Waterglass - check, the Egyptians knew how to make waterglass.
  2. Alkali activation - check, wood ash juice is a strong lye, which was also used for soap making.
  3. Metakaolin - what the heck is that?
Every ancient civilization with access to natron knew how to make waterglass. We see evidence of this everywhere (scoopmarks). And undoubtedly, they had access to wood ash. The question is whether they tried to mix the two. If they did, they could have created artificial stone. Let's recap what we know so far:
  • Molten natron etches quartz, consequently, granite
  • Etching granite produces waterglass
  • Waterglass is the main component of geopolymers
