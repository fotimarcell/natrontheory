The Scoop Marks Mystery

The Unfinished Obelisk in Assuan, Egypt

The unfinished obelisk is a massive, 40 meters long, 1100-ton granite block, whose carving was inexplicably halted by our ancestors. This has provided a great opportunity for us to examine how they worked.

It's certain that they didn't do it the way a modern person would. There are all sorts of strange processing marks around the obelisk.

And the famous diorite "pounding stones", of course.

The Scoop Marks Mystery

Around the unfinished obelisk, on the obelisk itself, and next to it in the trench, you can find distinctive depressions known as scoop marks.

According to the official explanation, these were created by diligent individuals using diorite pounding stones to carve them deeper and deeper.

What's wrong with this explanation?

This explanation has several issues. Not only is it incredibly difficult work, but in some places, like under the obelisk, it's downright impossible.

Secondly, it's entirely illogical to create nearly perfectly square depressions when one could make significant progress by simply knocking off the ridges. In fact, crushing the ridges would be "the" solution for this hard carving problem.

The Natron Theory

The natron theory states that our ancestors weren't foolish.

They didn't start carving granite with pounding stones if they knew that using molten natron made the work much easier. Once they had worked the surface with natron, carving became child's play.

They could definitely have used their pounding stones after etching the surface. In fact, that would have been a completely logical step, because they had to crush and remove the residue before a second round of etching.

What does the theory predict?

According to the theory, these depressions served the purpose of melting natron on the surface of the granite since molten natron is a liquid, and the edges were necessary to prevent it from spreading all over the area.

But how to heat the granite surface?

The challenge in heating the granite surface is that the heat dissipates. How did the Egyptians solve this problem?

Tomb of Rekhmire

There's a certain wall painting in the Metropolitan Museum, known as the Tomb of Rekhmire, that depicts metal smelting.

But if you spend some time analysing the picture, you realize this is the most idiotic way of doing any metal work.

Let's see what these guys are actually doing.

Melting iron on the floor?

Let's start with the upper left part. Here, two people are clearly sharpening fire with some tool - on the floor.

The problem with this is that the floor, like any large stone surface, almost sucks the heat out from underneath the tool. Not only is there a problem with what these good people will do if the metal accidentally melts on the floor, but also with the fact that they're deliberately making the work process more difficult by doing it on a challenging surface, reducing their chances of success.

Not to mention that what lurks beneath the embers doesn't even look like metal or ore.

Foot bellows

The tool at their feet is fairly easy to identify - it's a bellows, equipped with strings. The strings are used because, prior to the discovery of iron there were no springs. So, instead of springs, strings are used to reset the bellows to their original position after being stepped on.

In the pile, since the men are heating the floor, it's natron. They are using molten natron to etch the granite floor.

There's no evidence for this because there's no description accompanying the wall painting. If we say it's just a story, it's a much higher quality story anyway, than the official version about metal smelting.

Producing waterglass

If this story alone isn't convincing enough, let's take a different perspective. The natron theory suggests that at the site of the unfinished obelisk, and in various places around the world where scoop marks are found (Stonehenge, Peru, Sardinia, etc.), the primary goal wasn't actually stone carving.

After all, etching doesn't allow for precise cutting surfaces. Carving was just a secondary aspect.
The real activity in all these locations was the production of waterglass itself. And the end product was not the obelisk, but the waterglass. Let's recap what we know so far:
  • Molten natron etches quartz, consequently, granite
  • Etching granite produces waterglass
Ok, what the heck is waterglass? The very thing that make the third domino fall.
