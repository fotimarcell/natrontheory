Bottom, bottom, bottom?

What other characteristics should we pay attention to? So far, in every example we've examined, the nubs were at the bottom of the rock.

Let's look around the globe to see if the nubs are at the bottom in other locations as well.

At first glance, it seems we are in luck because the nubs on the Pyramid of Menkaure, which is only half a planet away, are also at the bottom.

Technical and functional nubs

Before we look at a fine example where the nubs are not at the bottom of the stone, let's introduce a new concept related to nubs. We will refer to those at the bottom of the stones as technical nubs.

Additionally, there are non-technical nubs that serve specific roles, possibly even encoding messages. I'm not dismissing this possibility. We will refer to these as functional nubs.

Technical nubs are a byproduct of stone-making technology and are essential for the integrity of the stone, similar to the nubs found in a Roman amphitheater in Turkey.

Nubs not at the bottom

Before you think we are selectively presenting certain types of nubs, let's see a good example where the nubs aren't on the bottom, yet we can still explain them later.

These nubs are lined up on one side of the Acropolis in Greece, with none on the lower side of the stone. What's going on here?

Let's set these aside for now; we'll return to them later and continue our discussion.

High precision

Another good question is how ancient stone carvers achieved such precision that not even a razor blade could be inserted between two stones.

This level of precision was certainly not achieved by lifting, carving here and there, and trying to put the 20-ton blocks back a dozen times. Such an approach would be madness.

Instead, they cast the stones directly into the wall, right next to the previous one. The heaviest item they would have needed to lift was merely a bucket.

(Oh, and nubs can be on the other side of the wall, of course.)

The role of technical nubs

The Water Problem

Although they knew the recipe for making stone, they were baffled as to why the material became soaking wet during mixing. Somehow, water was inexplicably entering the mix.

They simply couldn't control the amount of water in the mix. No matter how little water they added, many times that amount would appeared later on in the material.

Enough talking, let's see a concrete example!

Creating an Inca type stone from wood ash

Watch this video closely. You will see a hand mixing wood ash with waterglass.

It's important to note that very little waterglass is added, and the mix starts out almost bone dry. However, as he begins to stir and knead, the material becomes increasingly moist, eventually turning completely soggy.

"Something" added significant amount of water to the mix. Was it divine intervention?

What is a hydrate?

What happened here is that one of the components of the wood ash is a hydrate, containing water molecules embedded within. So the ash appears dry, feels dry - it's definitely dry, but...

However, one of its components secretly contains bound water molecules. These water molecules are held captive until the component itself transforms into another molecule.

At that moment, the previously held water molecules are released. You can't control this process, you can't undo it. And this is only the first hydrate in the mix...
The amount of water released from the ash is significant. However, what renders the struggle with excess water completely hopeless is the waterglass.

Waterglass as a hydrate?!

In the 21st century we purchase waterglass by the gallon at the hardware store. It's a liquid. Easy to handle, easy to control the amount of water, because that water is not hidden.

But what our ancestors produced through the ecthing of stones was solid waterglass powder. It's dry, but it's a hydrate. Specifically, a pentahydrate, where every waterglass molecule holds five (5!) water molecules captive.

Look, how the dry waterglass suddenly turns into a liquid! It's not melting, it's releasing hidden water!

1 drop

Do you know how much water is needed to cast a 20-ton stone with these two hidden water sources (wood ash and waterglass)? Just one drop. A single drop to start the reaction.

You can start kneading a small stone with one single drop of water, and if you handle it like a snowball, by gradually increasing its size, it will release enough water on its own to grow huge without any additional water.

We tried it, and this method works.

The Cascade

However, it's impractical for anyone to roll 20-ton stones to gradually increase their size, then lift them up and place them into their spot in the wall. That's a stupid idea.

Our ancestors had to add a specific initial amount of water to the stone mix and disperse it evenly; otherwise, the chemical process wouldn't start.

Every drop of water they initially added became excess water as the reaction began. And probably some more.
Mitigating the excess water problem

Strategy No. 1: The Nubs

It would be best to eliminate hidden water sources altogether, right?

That would solve all our problems at once. We'll get there, but for that, humanity has had to recognize the exact cause of the problem.

The Incas, and other peoples around the world, clearly did not realize this and therefore could do nothing more than leave a small hole on the mold for the excess water to escape.

Strategy No. 2: Waterglass solution

The next strategy might have been to recognize that the dry waterglass was not actually dry.

To eliminate this problem, it's sufficient to heat the waterglass powder until it transforms into a liquid, as shown in the pot above. This method ensures that the liquid waterglass alone is enough to initiate the chemical reaction; thus, there is no need to add extra water to the mix.

However, this still leaves the problem of the ash, though the amount of excess water is significantly reduced.

Strategy No. 3: replacing wood ash

However, the real solution comes from eliminating the other hidden water source: the wood ash.

Another advantage of this is that it also gets rid of the ugly gray color, assuming the replacement material is not gray.

The Egyptians were perhaps best positioned to achieve this step, as they had access to another source of aluminum for the mix: kaolin, a kind of clay that is the source material for all modern geopolymers.
Houston, we have a problem: the Acropolis!

Different stone families

First, let's consider the fact that the Acropolis is made of limestone, which belongs to a completely different rock family than quartz-based granite, basalt, etc.

Limestone is composed of the remnants of minuscule dead marine animals from billions of years ago, and is unrelated to waterglass or wood ash.

However, if we also find nubs here, could this suggest that limestone can also be produced artificially?

Artificial limestone

Yes, limestone can indeed be produced artificially, and this was demonstrated by none other than Joseph Davidovits, often referred to as the father of geopolymers.

In 2002, he created several 20-ton limestone blocks as evidence that the pyramids could also have been made from cast stones.

He used - surprise! - natron and a soft limestone to cast those blocks.

Another hydrate!

Davidovits used natron the way Imhotep did, by dissolving it in water. However, had he attempted to use it dry, adding water to the stone mixture afterwards, he would have been quite surprised.

The bone-dry natron, which can also be found in Wadi el Natron, is not merely a hydrate, but a decahydrate! Deca = 10.

It releases ten molecules of water during a chemical reaction. That's a heck of a lot of water! Could this have caused the great flood? ðŸ¤­

Nubs in the middle?

If an architect of a certain era, specifically an ancient Greek now, wanted to create stones using "dry" natron technology, then they definitely needed the nubs. Without draining the excess water, their stones would never have set. However, these nubs are not at the bottom!

Not only that, but the size of the blocks appear identical!

What's going on here?

Cast in a mold vs. cast in place

While the stones of the Inca walls were cast in place directly into the wall and were never moved again, the Greeks cast these stones into a mold, which explains their uniformity. (They were also smaller because they needed to be lifted afterwards.)

The nubs were turned outward when the stones were assembled into the wall at the Acropolis, to prevent the stones from wobbling against each other.

Indeed, the two techniques could also be combined. Let's take a look at a compelling example!

Naxos Portara

It would be hard to imagine a more beautiful ending. Take a look at this limestone gate and notice the nubs on it.

What's particularly interesting here is that while the two columns were cast standing up, possibly on site since the nubs are on the bottom of the column, the lintel was cast separately in a mold that included two drainage holes at each end.

After that, they rotated the finished lintel to ensure it wouldn't wobble and placed it on top of the columns. The two stones below were not cast in this position either; they were cast with the nubs facing downward.
