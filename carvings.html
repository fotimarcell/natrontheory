The huge ancient stone carvings have always amazed modern people. How did they do it? With copper chisels? That's impressive! A superhuman feat!

But what if our ancestors weren't that crazy and instead chose an easier method?

What if they knew about the beneficial effects of molten natron? That would be a completely different story. Let's see, how far is the nearest unlimited natron deposit from Petra! Why not?

What is that white patch?

Approximately 20 kilometers east of Petra, in the desert, lies a vast white expanse spanning several kilometers in diameter. What could this be?

It seems improbable that it's snow since this is not a high mountain region but one of the lowest areas on Earth. Limestone? No. Limestone typically forms mountains, not a spread scattered across the desert's surface.

That leaves us with salt. But what kind of salt?

Salt evaporation

For millennia, humanity has been producing the salt it needs through salt evaporation ponds. The principle here is that while the water evaporates and the pond slowly dries up, the salt remains at the bottom of the pond and crystallizes.

Why is this important to us? Because exactly the same thing happens in nature, with just a tiny twist. Those salt evaporation ponds aren't different colors by chance, but because...

Salts are different

There are various types of salts, with table salt (NaCl) being just one of them. Their common characteristics include the ability to dissolve in water and precipitate when the water evaporates.

However, they don't all precipitate at the same time. Some salts remain in the diminishing water much longer, while others precipitate in a rush. Salts have a precipitation order, with natron leading the way. It always precipitates first.

Precipitation order

So, in nature, this is what occurs: as a salty lake dries up and gradually recedes, it deposits the salts it contains onto its shores in the sequence of precipitation of salts. There will be a band of natron, followed by a band of such salts, then another type of salt, and finally, table salt remains at the very end. Here is a typical precipitation order:
  1. Natron (Sodium carbonate)
  2. Calcium sulfate
  3. Magnesium sulfate
  4. Potassium chloride
  5. Sodium chloride (Table salt)

Salinization

Now you can also figure out that if that spot there next to Petra is salt, then due to the precipitation sequence, it's almost certainly natron. The same thing happens everywhere in the world, where either the water or the soil is salty.

And it's important to note that it's enough for the soil to be salty. The lake may be freshwater, but ground water will eventually pump up sodium to the surface. This latter process is called salinization, and it will be important later on.

Any proof of ancient waterglass use?

Returning to the original mural, we can see a white pile of something accumulating in the background. That something is contaminated with black specks.

Of course, it's contaminated because it's mixed with the remaining charcoal. Then this white substance is carried around in bags, poured out, and someone molds a wall out of it.

All this is depicted in a singe mural (of smelting metal)!
Every ancient civilization with access to natron knew how to make waterglass. We see evidence of this everywhere (scoopmarks). And undoubtedly, they had access to wood ash. The question is whether they tried to mix the two. If they did, they could have created artificial stone. Let's recap what we know so far:
  • Molten natron etches quartz, consequently, granite
  • Etching granite produces waterglass
  • Waterglass is the main component of geopolymers
