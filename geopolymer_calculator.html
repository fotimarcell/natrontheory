The Kriven Formula

Precise geopolymer formulas are hard to find on the Internet. Not because it is high secret but because the field is still in development (=chaos). One of the leading scientists is Waltraud M. Kriven, we'll use her formula on this page for our calculations. This one:
M•AlO2•2SiO2•5.5H2O
Which looks absolutely crazy. Let me explain it in pure English.

Decyphering the formula

First of all, that capital M at the beginning of the formula is not a chemical element you never heard of but a placeholder for either sodium (Na) or potassium (K). Both can be used as alkali activators.

Second, if we omit that bloody oxigen (O) we end up with a much more comprehensible version:
M•Al•2 Si•5.5 water
Now we can try to decode the necessary parameters for our mixing.

What do we want to achieve?

Reading the above formula from left to right, our goal is:
  1. To have the same number of "M" (sodium or potassium) atoms as aluminum (Al) atoms in the mix
  2. To have twice as much silicon (Si) atoms than aluminum atoms
  3. To have 5.5 times more water molecules than aluminum atoms

Or in short we need

1 part "M", 1 part aluminum, 2 part silicon, 5.5 part water

Counting individual atoms?

As we cannot count individual atoms or molecules by pointing at them with a finger, we'll do the second best thing: we'll calculate the number of atoms in a material by knowig the mass of both the whole and the atomic/molar mass of the individual atoms/molecules.

You can get the exact number of molecules by simply dividing the weight of your material in grams with the molar mass of the given chemical. You'll get the result in "mol", or molar mass.

Calculating water's molecular mass

Atomic mass is the number of protons + neutrons in an atom, as both neutron's and proton's mass is 1 by definition. molecular mass is the sum of atomic masses in a molecule.

Hydrogen (H) has 1 proton only, so it's atomic mass is 1.
Oxigen (O) has 8 protons + 8 neutrons, so it's atomic mass is 16.

What is the molecular mass of water (H2O) then?
Well, it is 1 + 1 + 16 = 18

Step One
Calculating 1 part (1 mol) aluminum

Fixing a parameter: Aluminum

Our first step is to fix at least one parameter from the four above in order to keep our sanity. We'll still have 3 more variables, so we still face a 3-variable equation after we fix one of them.

Why Al first? Because there is only source of Al, which is our metakaloin. All the other elements are coming from two sources. (Sodium is found both in the waterglas and in NaOH for example.)

1 mol Al2O3 contains 2 mol (or 2 part) Aluminum atoms. But we only need 1 mol Aluminum so we need half mol Al2O3 in our mix. According to our table above, half mol of Al2O3's weight is
102 / 2 = 51 g

How many Metakaolin we need?

So to have 1 mol Aluminum we'll need 51 g of Al2O3 which is well mixed into your metakaolin. From this we can calculate the whole amount of metakaolin needed by using the percentage ratio of Al2O3. You will find this number in the product data sheet (probably a pdf).

Step Two
Calculating the other three components

The other three parameters

To fill in the gaps, we need to add some other materials to the mix:
  • Sodium (Na): from waterglass solution or from NaOH
  • Silicon (Si): from waterglass solution or from cat litter (not kidding)
  • Water: from waterglass solution or from the tap
Cat litter is an excellent source of water soluble, amorphous SiO2, it's easy to use, dirt cheap and clean. (If you use it directly from the bag, not from under the cat.)
